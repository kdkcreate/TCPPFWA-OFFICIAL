Sensor Documentation

DHT11 Sensor

The DHT11 sensor is a widely used, low-cost digital device that measures temperature and relative humidity. It combines a capacitive humidity sensor and a thermistor to produce a calibrated digital output signal.

Working Principle

The DHT11 sensor contains a humidity sensing component that measures the moisture content in the air by changing electrical resistance capacitively. The thermistor measures temperature by varying resistance with heat. An internal chip processes these analog signals into a digital form which is sent over a single-wire serial interface to microcontrollers.

Specifications

  • Operating voltage: 3.3V to 5V DC
  • Temperature range: 0 to 50 °C
  • Temperature accuracy: ±2 °C
  • Humidity range: 20% to 90% RH
  • Humidity accuracy: ±5% RH
  • Sampling rate: Once every 1 second
  • Communication protocol: Single-wire digital serial interface

Advantages

  • Simple to interface with microcontrollers (Arduino, ESP32, ESP8266, Raspberry Pi)
  • Low power consumption
  • Compact and low cost
  • Reliable digital data output

Limitations

  • Lower accuracy compared to more advanced sensors (e.g., DHT22)
  • Narrower temperature and humidity ranges
  • Slow sampling rate unsuitable for rapid environmental changes

Applications

Used in weather stations, HVAC systems, environmental monitoring, agriculture, and IoT projects requiring temperature and humidity data.

Integration Tips

  • Use a pull-up resistor (typically 4.7kΩ) on the data line for stable communication.
  • Ensure power supply voltage is stable (3.3V or 5V depending on your microcontroller).
  • Use libraries like the Adafruit DHT library for easy coding.
  • Place the sensor away from heat sources and direct sunlight to avoid false readings.

Troubleshooting

  • Erratic readings may indicate loose wiring or insufficient power.
  • Check timing in your code — sensor requires specific timing to read data.
  • High humidity readings may occur if sensor is close to water or condensation.

Ultrasonic Sensor (HC-SR04)

The ultrasonic sensor measures distance by emitting high-frequency sound pulses (~40kHz) and measuring the time it takes for the echo to return after bouncing off an object.

Working Principle

The sensor has a transmitter that emits ultrasonic waves and a receiver that listens for their reflection. The time delay between sending and receiving the pulse is used to calculate the distance based on the speed of sound (~343 m/s at room temperature).

Specifications

  • Operating voltage: 5V DC
  • Measuring range: 2 cm to 400 cm (0.8 inch to 13 feet)
  • Accuracy: ±3 mm
  • Angle of detection: Approximately 15 degrees
  • Output: Digital pulse width corresponding to distance

Advantages

  • Non-contact measurement
  • Wide range of detection
  • Relatively inexpensive
  • Easy to interface with most microcontrollers

Limitations

  • Performance affected by environmental noise and temperature
  • Cannot detect very soft or angled surfaces well (sound may scatter)
  • Requires line of sight

Applications

Obstacle detection, robotics, parking sensors, level measurement, and in your case, wildlife proximity detection.

Integration Tips

  • Use a 5V supply; if your microcontroller uses 3.3V logic (like ESP32), use a logic level converter for signal lines.
  • Trigger pin needs a 10µs HIGH pulse to start measurement.
  • Measure the pulse width on the echo pin to calculate distance.

Troubleshooting

  • False readings may come from objects out of sensor range or soft materials.
  • Ensure there’s no obstruction or noise near the sensor.
  • Wiring issues or power instability can cause erratic behavior.

Sound Sensor

The sound sensor detects sound intensity levels in the environment by converting acoustic signals into electrical signals.

Working Principle

Typically uses a microphone to pick up ambient sounds, then amplifies and processes the signal to determine sound levels. The sensor outputs an analog voltage proportional to the sound intensity or a digital signal if a threshold is crossed.

Specifications

  • Operating voltage: 3.3V to 5V
  • Output: Analog voltage or digital HIGH/LOW output
  • Sensitivity: Adjustable via onboard potentiometer on many modules

Advantages

  • Simple and low cost
  • Can detect abnormal or loud sounds
  • Easy integration with microcontrollers

Limitations

  • Cannot distinguish sound types (just volume)
  • Susceptible to environmental noise
  • Needs calibration for threshold sensitivity

Applications

Noise detection, sound level monitoring, security alarms, and your project’s use for detecting unusual wildlife sounds.

Integration Tips

  • Connect the output pin to an ADC pin if analog readings are needed.
  • Adjust sensitivity via potentiometer for your environment.
  • Use software filtering to ignore background noise.

Troubleshooting

  • False triggers from loud but irrelevant noises.
  • Check power stability and wiring.
  • Use shielding or noise reduction techniques if needed.

Gas/Smoke Sensors (MQ Series)

Gas and smoke sensors detect harmful gases such as carbon monoxide, methane, LPG, or smoke particles, providing early warning of fire hazards or environmental threats.

Working Principle

Most common gas sensors (e.g., MQ-2, MQ-5) use a heated semiconductor sensor whose resistance changes when it comes into contact with certain gases. This change in resistance is converted to an analog voltage signal proportional to gas concentration.

Specifications

  • Operating voltage: 5V DC
  • Detection gases: Varies by model (e.g., MQ-2 detects smoke, propane, methane)
  • Output: Analog voltage
  • Preheat time: Typically 20 seconds to stabilize

Advantages

  • Affordable and easy to use
  • Detects multiple types of gases
  • Provides analog output for flexible thresholding

Limitations

  • Requires warm-up time before accurate readings
  • Sensor aging and contamination can affect accuracy
  • Not highly selective; cross-sensitivity to other gases

Applications

Fire detection, industrial safety, air quality monitoring, and detecting human interference risks in wildlife habitats.

Integration Tips

  • Use a dedicated ADC or ensure your microcontroller’s ADC has adequate resolution.
  • Calibrate sensor periodically with known gas concentrations if possible.
  • Allow sensor to warm up after powering on before taking readings.

Troubleshooting

  • Unstable readings if warm-up time is insufficient.
  • False positives from environmental gases or humidity changes.
  • Check sensor connections and power supply stability.

Board Integration

The ESP8266/ESP32 is used to connect sensors and transmit data over Wi-Fi. It communicates with platforms like Adafruit IO or Blynk using MQTT or HTTP.

Key Notes:

  • Ensure the board has adequate power via an external power module.
  • Use GPIO pins carefully to avoid conflicts (e.g., avoid GPIO0, GPIO2 for pull-ups).
  • Install required libraries like Adafruit_Sensor, DHT, PubSubClient or BlynkSimpleEsp8266.

Use resistors for pull-up/down logic where necessary, especially for analog sensors.

Troubleshooting

Problem: ESP8266 not connecting to Wi-Fi.
Solution: Double check SSID/password. Ensure board is close to the router. Use 2.4GHz band.

Problem: Sensors not reading values.
Solution: Check power and ground lines. Ensure pins match with the code. Add delays where needed to allow sensor response.

Problem: Data not showing on Blynk/Adafruit IO.
Solution: Confirm token/username/AIO key. Recheck feed/topic names.
